description: |
  The Macaulay Library and other biodiversity media collections (e.g., iNaturalist) house "media specimens" (photos, videos and audio recordings) that capture the phenotypic traits of individuals, including behavioral traits. Digitization of the Macaulay Collection began well over two decades ago, and today most new assets are coming to us digitally-borne; there are no physical objects. These two factors make the Macaulay collection quite different from many other biological collections, most of which curate physical specimens and began digitization efforts more recently. Yet the lessons learned by the Macaulay digitization efforts can inform those of other collections. We have found that digitization has led to important benefits for our collection and our mission, particularly when combined with machine learning and other AI approaches. These benefits include increased collections growth and accessibility, improved data quality, and new opportunities for research as well as educational outreach. Many of these benefits are agnostic about the specimen type (i.e., media versus physical). At the same time, AI presents new challenges for media collections, requiring new safeguards that we never dreamed would be needed a decade ago. In this workshop I will share some of the benefits and concerns that we have discovered for our collection, discuss how other collections with physical specimens might (or might not) face similar benefits or challenges, and brainstorm about what the future might hold.
