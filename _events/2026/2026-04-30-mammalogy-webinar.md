description: |
  Beginning in the 1970s, mammal collections began the process of digitizing their paper catalogs using mainframe computers. By the 1990s, mammal collections shifted their specimen cataloging to electronic databases, which soon led to the phasing out of paper catalogs. Georeferencing and data aggregation efforts, such as the Mammal Networked Information System (MaNIS), soon followed, placing mammalogy and its natural history collections ahead of the digitization curve by the early 2000s. Recognizing the need for similar digitization efforts across all natural history collections, the National Science Foundation established the Advancing Digitization of Biodiversity Collections (ADBC) program and created iDigBio as the central coordinating organization, funding projects to digitize and mobilize data in many botanical and invertebrate collections. Because of the extent of their taxonomic diversity, these collections were prioritized over mammal and other vertebrate collections. As these biodiverse collections began to mobilize their specimen data, attention returned to vertebrate collections as ideas of the "extended specimen" began take hold, leading to ambitious digitization projects, such oVert, that reinvigorated some scientific fields (e.g., anatomy) and expanded collection use in others (e.g., biomechanics). Additional mammal-focused digitization efforts have focused on expanding the availability of x-ray computed tomography (CT) datasets (i.e., FuncQEE and BatPEN!) and lifting trait data (i.e., Ranges). These digitization efforts, along with long-term investments in maintaining rigorous taxonomic and database resources, have positioned mammalogy to be a leader in digital collections. However, as technology continues to develop, more opportunities to expand digitization efforts in mammalogy will arise.
